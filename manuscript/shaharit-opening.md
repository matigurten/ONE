# ONE - Contemporary Opening: Shaharit

## Fima's Morning

---

The alarm went off at three in the morning. He picked up the phone before he was awake — not to check anything specific, just the reflex, the screen, the confirmation that the world was still there. It was. He put it down and got up.

Pee. Teeth. Floss. Shower. Then the documents: passport, confirmation, transfer details, the address of the pousada written on a note in his wallet in case the phone died. All there. All in order.

The house was quiet. He came out of his room and walked past the living room — his seventeen year old sister's sweater on the sofa, the one with a pop star's name on it. He picked it up, farted on it, and put it back. That singer sucks.

He went to the kitchen, turned on the capsule machine and waited for the water to heat. On the counter, a fifty euro bill with a Post-it on each half. The left one: *Have fun in Brazil! There is breakfast in the fridge. Love mom and dad xoxo. PS: be careful. Take the taxi.* The right one: blank. A pen laid across the middle.

Not a suggestion.

He picked up the pen and wrote on the blank half: *Will be! See you in two weeks!!! Thanks for everything guys, you rock.* Peeled it off and pressed it to the counter where the bill had been. He took the bill — his mother's Post-it still attached — and slid it into his wallet. Her blessing went with it. Be careful, she had written. The universe had been notified. He was protected.

He opened the fridge.

He made a Nespresso with milk and sat down. Scrolled through Instagram — photos of Jericoacoara, the dunes, the lagoons, the sand streets. Not really looking, just idling through them. Three in the morning and the excitement was there underneath somewhere but his body wasn't ready for it yet. Saving everything for later.

His phone chimed. The screen showed two minutes for the taxi.

He opened WhatsApp and typed *awake?* and sent it to Alex.

No answer.

A rush of adrenaline flooded his body.

---

— It is on, he said out loud.

There was no one to hear it.

Passport. Phone. Wallet. Three times he checked.

The backpack was by the front door where he had left it the night before. Everything in its place, the smaller pouch sitting on top. It looked like it had been packed by an excited child before the first day of kindergarten.

He put it on and opened the front door.

## Alex's Morning

---

At three fifty his phone lit up. A WhatsApp from Fima. *awake?*
He didn't see it.

The alarm went off at four. He hit snooze.

It went off again at four ten. He hit snooze.

At four twenty it rang one last time. He sat on the edge of the bed and turned it off. He had risen.

He went to the bathroom and turned on the shower. He stood in front of the mirror while the water ran. His face looked back at him. After a while he got in, peed, brushed his teeth. Then he stood under the water with his face turned up toward the head. He got out and dried himself with the towel on the hook. It was damp. It wasn't his. He didn't give this a second thought.

He went to the kitchen. The apartment was quiet — the others asleep. He made a coffee, took it back to his room, and drank it sitting on the edge of the bed. The mixing console took up most of the desk the way it always did, cables off the back of it going nowhere in particular.

The backpack was in the corner. He opened it. A few shirts from the chair, whichever ones came off first. Shorts. A second pair. Sandals pushed down the side. He looked at the bag for a moment, decided it was fine, and zipped it.

He found his headphones under the bed and put them around his neck. His keys were on the desk. His passport was under them.

He picked up both, pulled the door shut behind him, and went down the stairs.

## Carolina's Morning

---

The alarm went off at six. She lay there for a moment the way she always did — not asleep, not quite awake, listening to the ocean through the wood slats of her window. After more than a year in this room she still wasn't tired of that sound. Some mornings it was the only reason getting up felt reasonable.

She used the bathroom, splashed water on her face, pulled her hair back. Put on the clothes she'd left on the chair the night before and went downstairs.

The place was hers at this hour. No guests, no sounds except the birds starting up in the trees above and the kitchen coming alive under her hands. Coffee first, always coffee first, then the fruit. Watermelon, papaya, tomatoes cut into chunks. Thick slices of bread for the toast. Butter, jam, condensed milk on the side. By seven the tables were set — crocheted placemats, everything simple and in its place — and she poured herself a cup and sat in one of the low chairs in the garden and had ten minutes of nothing, which was the best ten minutes of the day.

The garden was red earth under the trees, the big ones that held the whole place up, their roots pushing through the ground and their branches holding the thatched roof of the lobby up in the canopy like a nest. A tipi on one side. Steps cut into the base of the largest tree leading up to the rooms above. Flags hanging between the branches, barely moving. In the morning light, before anyone was awake, it was the best place she had ever lived and she knew it.

## Amber's Morning

---

She woke before him. She always woke before him.

The treehouse was already warm, the kind of warm that arrives before you open your eyes and tells you exactly where you are. Through the wooden slats the ocean was doing what the ocean does — steady, indifferent, already at full volume. A bird she didn't know the name of was making a sound she didn't have a word for. The red earth smell came up through the heat.

She lay there for a moment. Not thinking. Just taking inventory — the body checking in with itself the way it does in the morning, the way it does before a session on the water, the quiet competent assessment of a person who has spent years trusting what her body tells her. Everything present. Everything fine.

She got up without waking him.

Toilet. Teeth. The shower was lukewarm and she didn't mind — she had stopped minding lukewarm showers sometime around her second season on the water, when the cold stopped being something that happened to her and became something she moved through. She stood under it for exactly as long as she needed to and not a second longer.

She dressed in the half dark. Shorts, a loose shirt, the sandals she'd left by the door. Picked up her hair with one hand and let it go. Good enough.

The path from the residential area to the breakfast garden was short and red and root-crossed, the big trees holding everything overhead, the flags between the branches hanging still in the morning air. She walked it the way she walked everything — unhurried, completely present, her feet finding the ground without looking down.

At the entrance to the garden a man was already at his table with a coffee and what looked like a phone or a notebook — hard to tell. He looked up when she came in.

Good morning, he said.

Good morning, she said.

She turned back toward the treehouse.

— Ken! Breakfast!

---

The garden absorbed the sound. She found a table. Carolina appeared with coffee. The morning continued.

## Ken's Morning

---

She was already up. He heard her call from somewhere outside — breakfast, finishing soon — and didn't answer. But his eyes were open.

He had arrived the night before. The same nineteen hours, the same dunes, the same sand streets in the dark. He had fallen into the bed and slept without moving. That was yesterday. Today was already something else.

He got up. Peed. Looked at himself briefly in the mirror — hair, face, the general situation — and found nothing requiring attention. Shirt from the chair. Swim shorts from the floor. He went downstairs.

She was at the table with two coffees. He sat down. Ate. Looked at the garden — the red earth, the big trees holding everything up, the light coming through in patches. A man at the other table eating two full breakfasts.

He brushed his teeth after the coffee. Then they went to find the beach.
