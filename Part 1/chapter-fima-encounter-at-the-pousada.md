# Fima's Chapter — Encounter at the Pousada

---

## ♩ 120 BPM
*🎵 Miss Monique — put it on before you read*

The alarm went off at four in the morning. I picked up the phone before I was awake — not to check anything specific, just the reflex, the screen, the confirmation that the world was still there. It was. I put it down and got up.

Pee. Teeth. Floss. Shower. Then the documents: passport, confirmation, transfer details, the address of the pousada written on a note in my wallet in case the phone died. All there. All in order.

The backpack was already packed. I'd done it the night before, everything in its place, the smaller pouch sitting on top. I'd left it by the door before going to sleep. Standing there at four in the morning picking it up, I felt like an excited kid before his first day of kindergarten.

At four in the morning the house was quiet. I came out of my room and walked past the living room — Chiara's sweater on the sofa, the one with a pop star's name on it. I picked it up, opened the narrow closet next to the kitchen where the wifi router lives, put it inside, and closed the door. Teasing her was second nature after seventeen years of being her brother, but this? Honestly, I was just helping. That singer sucks.

I went to the kitchen, turned on the capsule machine and waited for the water to heat, then opened the fridge.

On the counter, a fifty euro bill with a Post-it on each half. The left one: *Have fun in Brazil! There is breakfast in the fridge. Love mom and dad xoxo. PS: be careful. Take the taxi.* The right one: blank. A pen laid across the middle.

Not a suggestion.

I picked up the pen and wrote on the blank half: *Will be! See you in two weeks!!! Thanks for everything guys, you rock.* Peeled it off and pressed it to the counter where the bill had been. Took the bill — her blessing still on it — and slid it into my wallet. For a second, holding it, it felt like the blessing of a queen before her prince goes out into the world. It stayed in my wallet the whole trip.

I made a Nespresso with milk and sat down. Scrolled through Instagram — photos of Jericoacoara, the dunes, the lagoons, the sand streets. Not really looking, just idling through them. Four in the morning and the excitement was there underneath somewhere but my body wasn't ready for it yet. Saving everything for later.

---

The pousada had a central garden with a table under a mango tree where breakfast happened every morning at eight. Fresh fruit. Tapioca. Someone's grandmother's recipe for something involving coconut that I didn't catch the name of. You sat down, the food appeared, and if you were there long enough you started to recognize the other guests by their order.

I'd been there three days when I noticed him.

He was at the far end of the table with a coffee and what looked like a phone or a notebook — hard to tell from where I was sitting — and he was talking to himself. Not quietly. Not the way people do when they're reading aloud by accident. He was working something out in real time, following a thought wherever it went, occasionally stopping to write something down, then continuing. The others at the table had done the polite thing and redistributed themselves to the other end.

I sat down in the middle.

His name was Mati. He introduced himself when he noticed me not moving away, which seemed to register as unusual enough to interrupt the monologue. Israeli. Tel Aviv. Something in finance, though he said it the way people say *something in finance* when finance isn't really the point.

He asked where I was from. I told him. He nodded like that explained something, though I couldn't tell what.

Then he said: *Can I show you something?*

I said sure, because I had nowhere to be and the coconut thing was very good and I was on my second one.

He put his phone on the table between us and turned it so I could see the screen. A spreadsheet. Columns: genre, BPM, height in meters, height in centimeters, something called *energia*. Rows: House, Techno, Trance, Psytrance, High Tech.

I looked at it for a moment.

*What am I looking at?*

*The physics of a party,* he said. Like it was obvious. Like I'd asked what two plus two was.

He explained it the way people explain things when they've been explaining it mostly to themselves for a while and have gotten very good at the explanation but have temporarily forgotten that not everyone has been following along from the beginning. Fast, confident, not unkind. The BPM of a genre determines the period of a beat. The period determines how long you're in the air between steps. The height of your jump is a function of that time and gravity. Different genres correspond to different jump heights. Different planets have different gravities. Therefore —

He stopped.

---

*Therefore what?* I asked.

*Therefore the genre that feels right depends on where you are.* He picked the phone back up. *On Earth, psytrance puts you at twenty-three centimeters. That's the sweet spot. That's where the body wants to be. But on Mars —* he scrolled to another tab — *gravity is 3.71. Same BPM, same beat, you'd be jumping nine centimeters. That's not a party. That's a shuffle.* He said this with genuine distaste, the way a person talks about food that has disappointed them.

I looked at the spreadsheet again. The math was right, as far as I could tell. The logic was internally consistent. The conclusion was completely insane.

*You're calculating the optimal music genre for dancing on other planets.*

*For any gravitational environment,* he said. *The formula generalizes. You pick your target jump height, you know your gravity, you solve for BPM.* He paused. *The DJ is responsible for this. If people aren't dancing, it's the DJ's fault. The DJ chose the wrong frequency for the room.*

I ate some more of the coconut thing. A rooster somewhere in the garden made a noise and stopped.

*What do you do with this?* I asked. *Professionally, I mean.*

He thought about it. Not evasively — genuinely, like the question had a real answer he was trying to locate.

*I think,* he said slowly, *it goes somewhere bigger. The resonance part. The frequency part. I don't know yet where exactly.* He looked at the phone. *But it's not about planets. That's just how I found it.*

He told me then about the desert. The Judean hills, a party that didn't officially exist, a very specific batch of something a friend had described as unlocking things. The moon coming up red over the rocks. His body moving without deciding to, and the equations arriving fully formed, like something he was remembering rather than inventing.

I've heard people talk about epiphanies before. Usually it sounds like self-mythologizing — the story gets cleaner each time they tell it, the insight grander, the circumstances more cinematic. This didn't sound like that. It sounded like a man describing something that had happened to him that he was still slightly puzzled by. The math came first, he said. The meaning was still coming.

*You think frequencies can do something,* I said. *Beyond making you dance.*

*I think they already do,* he said. *I just don't have the vocabulary for it yet.*

---

The table had mostly emptied by then. The grandmother's coconut thing was gone. Mati's coffee had been cold for a while and he hadn't noticed.

I didn't know what to do with any of it. I'm not a physicist. I'm not a mystic. I'm a person who woke up at four in the morning and flew to Brazil because the photos of the dunes looked like something worth seeing, and I was sitting across from a man who had apparently worked out the correct BPM for dancing on Mars and believed it was the beginning of something larger.

Part of me thought: this guy is a very specific kind of crazy. The kind that does real math in service of a conclusion no sane person would reach. I've met a few. There's something almost admirable about it.

But the formula was right. I checked it on my own phone while he was talking. h = (30/bpm)² · (a/2). Plug in the numbers. They come out the way he said they do.

That's the part that stuck.

Not the planets. Not the desert. Just the fact that the numbers were right, and he'd found them dancing alone in the dark with his eyes closed, following a frequency his body understood before his brain did.

I don't know what that means. Maybe nothing. Maybe he'll go back to Tel Aviv and the spreadsheet will sit on his phone for years and eventually he'll stop thinking about it.

Or maybe the vocabulary will come.

I kept thinking about it the rest of the trip. On the dunes. In the lagoon. On the flight home when I couldn't sleep and the cabin was dark and the engine noise was a low, even frequency somewhere around 80 BPM, not quite enough to dance to, just enough to feel.
