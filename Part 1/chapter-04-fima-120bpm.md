# Chapter 4 — Fima (120 BPM)
## ♩ 120 BPM
*🎵 Miss Monique — put it on before you read*

---

The alarm went off at four in the morning. I picked up the phone before I was awake — not to check anything specific, just the reflex, the screen, the confirmation that the world was still there. It was. I put it down and got up.

Pee. Teeth. Floss. Shower. Then the documents: passport, confirmation, transfer details, the address of the pousada written on a note in my wallet in case the phone died. All there. All in order.

The backpack was already packed. I'd done it the night before, everything in its place, the smaller pouch sitting on top. I'd left it by the door before going to sleep. Standing there at four in the morning picking it up, I felt like an excited kid before his first day of kindergarten.

At four in the morning the house was quiet. I came out of my room and walked past the living room — Chiara's sweater on the sofa, the one with a pop star's name on it. I picked it up, opened the narrow closet next to the kitchen where the wifi router lives, put it inside, and closed the door. Teasing her was second nature after seventeen years of being her brother, but this? Honestly, I was just helping. That singer sucks.

I went to the kitchen, turned on the capsule machine and waited for the water to heat, then opened the fridge.

On the counter, a fifty euro bill with a Post-it on each half. The left one: *Have fun in Brazil! There is breakfast in the fridge. Love mom and dad xoxo. PS: be careful. Take the taxi.* The right one: blank. A pen laid across the middle.

Not a suggestion.

I picked up the pen and wrote on the blank half: *Will be! See you in two weeks!!! Thanks for everything guys, you rock.* Peeled it off and pressed it to the counter where the bill had been. Took the bill — her blessing still on it — and slid it into my wallet. For a second, holding it, it felt like the blessing of a queen before her prince goes out into the world. It stayed in my wallet the whole trip.

I made a Nespresso with milk and sat down. Scrolled through Instagram — photos of Jericoacoara, the dunes, the lagoons, the sand streets. Not really looking, just idling through them. Four in the morning and the excitement was there underneath somewhere but my body wasn't ready for it yet. Saving everything for later.

I read her note again. Used the pen to write back on the same piece of paper. Told her not to worry, sent kisses to dad. Left it on the counter.

---

The map showed six minutes for the taxi. Something hit the spine — adrenaline, finally — and I was on.

Passport. Phone. Documents. Backpack by the door. Three times through, all there.

The phone chimed. *Taxi is outside.*

It is on, I said to myself.

I opened the front door.

---

Nineteen hours later I was standing in a shower in Jericoacoara with the red dust of the dunes washing off my feet and the sound of the ocean coming through the window and I felt, completely and without qualification, that I had made it.

I dried off, put on clean clothes, and went to find the lobby.

---

I had a coffee and a croissant at the terminal, then a cheese sandwich on baguette before boarding. The coffee was good. I hoped it would be as good in Brazil.

I watched films. I ate what they brought. Somewhere over the Atlantic I fell into something that wasn't quite sleep.

Fortaleza from the air looked like the edge of the world. We landed in the afternoon, the heat arriving the moment the doors opened — thicker than Paris, more present, the kind that has an opinion about you. A transfer van for five hours across the northeast, the landscape going from highway to smaller road to smaller road again, the light turning orange then red.

Then Jijoca. The asphalt stopped. A man with a 4x4 was waiting with a piece of paper.

---

The last hour was the dunes.

I don't have the right words for the dunes. The vehicle climbing sand that looked like it couldn't hold weight, cresting ridges with nothing visible on the other side until suddenly there was everything — the ocean, the lagoons, the low town materializing out of the landscape like it had always been there and the rest of the world was the strange part. No roads. No cars. Sand streets, the sound of the ocean somewhere close, flags and hammocks and music coming from open doorways.

We found the pousada. Carolina gave us our key. We dropped everything and went out.

An hour, maybe a little more. Walked the sand streets, ate something at a place with plastic chairs outside, watched the town doing its evening. It was warm and easy and after nineteen hours of travel it felt like arriving somewhere that had been waiting.

Alex slept. Of course Alex slept.

At nine we found the lobby.

---

The fire was going and there were people around it and music was playing that I hadn't heard before — no voice, no lyrics, just something moving forward at a steady pace that felt different from what you usually hear in a place like this. Cleaner. More deliberate. A man I didn't know was talking, leaning forward in his chair, and the English couple from breakfast — Carolina had mentioned them — were listening with the particular attention of people who have been pulled somewhere they didn't plan to go.

Alex found two chairs at the edge of the fire. We sat down.

I didn't catch the beginning. I caught the middle — something about music and parties and what fun actually was, broken down with a precision that felt slightly absurd and completely serious at the same time. The man was Israeli, I thought, from the accent. Older than us. He talked the way people talk when they've been thinking about something for a long time and have finally found an audience.

Carolina brought us drinks without being asked. I mouthed thank you across the fire.

At some point the man asked us — me and Alex — what we did for fun. How we had fun. What fun actually was when you broke it down.

Alex said music. I said football.

The man looked at me the way a teacher looks at a student who has accidentally given the right answer to a different question than the one being asked. He used it — took my football and built something with it, the way he seemed to build with everything that was handed to him.

We eat, he said. We sleep. We work. We exercise. All of these have goals, functions, reasons. Having fun is different — it's its own category, its own activity with its own logic. And what do we do to have fun? We party. So what is a party? What are we actually trying to do?

He talked about frequency. About the difference between music that needs another person and music that doesn't. About the party as a biological trap — the most irresistible version of fun attached to the thing evolution needed most. I thought about the clubs back home. The indoor ones, dark, loud, the specific energy of a hundred people in an enclosed space all wanting the same thing without saying so. The music at 120, maybe a little more — no lyrics usually, but still with that pull toward something outside yourself. Still horizontal, he would have said, though I didn't have that word yet.

I'd felt it sometimes at those parties — not often, not reliably — a moment when the music stopped being about the room and started being about something else. Something inward. I never knew what to do with it. It would come and go and I'd lose it in the crowd and the noise and whatever happened next.

I didn't say any of this. I just listened.
