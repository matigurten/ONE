# Chapter 4 — Amber

---

We had arrived the night before, late, the kind of late where you don't really see a place so much as fall into it. I remembered the sand road giving way to red earth, the warm dark air, lanterns hanging between enormous trees. I remembered thinking it was beautiful and then being asleep almost immediately.

The morning had been slow and good. Breakfast in the open garden — thick toast, fruit, coffee that came with condensed milk on the side which I hadn't expected and loved immediately. The keeper, Carolina, Argentine, moved between the tables without fuss. Ken had spent the afternoon at the beach. I had spent it in a hammock strung between two of the big trees, with a book I didn't read, watching the light move through the canopy, which is my preferred way to spend an afternoon.

By early evening we had found the lobby.

It was built into the base of the largest tree — or rather the tree had grown through it, the roots pushing up through the red earth floor, the branches spreading overhead into a thatched nest of a roof. Open on all sides to the garden. A fire going in a stone pit, low chairs arranged around it, flags hanging between the branches outside. The kind of place designed for sitting quietly and not being anywhere in particular. I was looking forward to being nowhere in particular.

The music was not designed for that.

It wasn't coming from anywhere I could see — speakers hidden somewhere in the structure — and it wasn't unpleasant exactly. I couldn't explain what was wrong with it. It just felt like the wrong thing for this moment, for this place, for the kind of evening I had in mind. Too urgent. Too directed. Like someone else's agenda playing at full volume in a room you'd chosen for its quiet.

I stopped walking.

The music was loud. Not concert loud, not damaging, but loud in a way that felt like it had opinions about you. A relentless thumping thing, all pulse and urgency, the kind of music that seems to be going somewhere very fast and expects you to keep up. After eleven hours of recycled airplane air and two hours of potholed road I was in no condition to keep up with anything.

*What is this,* I thought. *And why.*

It wasn't unpleasant exactly. I couldn't explain what was wrong with it. It just felt like the wrong thing for this moment, for this place, for someone who had only just arrived and hadn't yet worked out where she was. Like being handed a large meal the moment you step off a plane. The body just isn't ready.

---

Ken had already found a chair by the fire. Ken can adapt to any environment within ninety seconds. It is one of the things I love about him and also one of the things that make me want to shake him by the shoulders.

I found a chair next to him. The music continued its residency in my skull.

A woman appeared — early thirties, Argentine from the accent when she spoke, dark hair pulled back, the particular unhurried competence of someone who has run this place long enough to have seen every variety of arriving traveler and remains unimpressed by all of them.

She asked if we wanted anything.

I asked for two caipirinhas. Ken looked up from the fire and smiled without turning his head, which is something he does, and which works on me every time, infuriatingly.

The woman — Carolina, she said, when I asked — went to make the drinks and I watched her go and then turned to Ken and mouthed *the music* and made a face that I hoped conveyed my full position on the subject. He made a face back that conveyed *it's not that bad* which it absolutely was.

When Carolina came back with the drinks I asked, as carefully as I could manage, whether the music was always like this.

She looked at me. Not unkindly. The look of someone who has fielded this question before.

I said it was a bit much. For this environment. For this hour. For my particular skull at this particular moment.

She nodded once and went somewhere and the music changed — dropped, shifted, became something with acoustic guitar and a voice and a rhythm like breathing, Brazilian, warm, like the air itself had decided to have an opinion. Forró, I would learn later. The music of the northeast. The music that sounds like exactly where you are.

My skull relaxed.

Ken reached over without looking and touched my hand.

I was nearly asleep in the chair when the man appeared.

He came from inside the main structure — down a staircase, barefoot, moving at an ordinary pace but somehow differently from how people ordinarily move. It took me a moment to identify what was different. It was his attention. It wasn't directed at anything in particular — not at the fire, not at us, not at the music — and yet it seemed to be taking everything in simultaneously, the whole room registering in him without any apparent effort, the way a place registers in someone who has already learned it by heart except he had only just arrived in it. His mind, I thought, was completely on. Whatever that means. It was just the impression I had.

Israeli, I thought, from something about the way he carried himself, though I couldn't have explained the reasoning. Late twenties maybe, early thirties at most. Eyes that were doing a great deal of work.

He stopped when he heard the music.

He stood there for a moment, his head tilted slightly, and I could see him registering the change — the forró where something else had been — and looking around the room for an explanation. His gaze moved across the fire, across Ken, across the empty chairs, and landed on me.

He came over.

He asked, quite directly and without any of the usual social preamble, whether I was the one who had asked for the music to change.

I said yes. I said it had been a bit harsh for me.

He sat down in the chair across from me — not asking, exactly, but not not asking — and looked at me with an expression I couldn't entirely read and said:

*What kind of music do you like?*

And I said — without thinking, because I was exhausted and my filters were all offline and it was simply the true answer —

*Everything.*

The effect was immediate and theatrical and completely unexpected.

He grabbed the front of his shirt with both hands, directly over his heart, and reeled backward in his chair as if I had shot him. Not a gentle flinch. A full-body dramatic stagger, the kind you'd do on a stage so the people in the back row could see.

*Everything?!*

He said it the way you'd say it if someone had told you the moon was made of cheese and they were perfectly serious. Equal parts delight and outrage.

*How is that possible.*

---

He leaned forward now, the performance dropping into something more genuine but no less intense.

*Most of it sucks.*

I opened my mouth. I closed it. I genuinely did not know what to say to this man who had appeared from a staircase and was now treating my listening habits as a matter of some urgency.

From the fireplace, without turning around, Ken said:

*If you can figure out what she likes, I'd love to know.*

He said it warmly, the way you say something that's true and funny and slightly at someone's expense when you love them. I gave the back of his head a look he couldn't see.

The man looked at Ken. Then back at me. Something shifted in his expression — a decision being made, or maybe the recognition that a decision had already been made somewhere upstream and he was only now catching up to it.

He settled back into his chair.

*Okay,* he said. *Let's start from the beginning.*

He said it the way people say things when they mean it. Not *let's start from the beginning* as a figure of speech. *Let's start from the beginning* as a genuine proposal, as a thing he intended to do, as if beginning at the beginning was the only reasonable response to a person who liked everything.

The fire crackled. The forró played softly. The palm trees moved in the coastal wind outside the open walls.

I looked at Ken. Ken was looking at the man now, turned in his chair, one eyebrow slightly raised in the expression that means *this should be interesting.*

I looked back at the man.

I still didn't know his name. I wouldn't ask for another hour.

*Okay,* I said.
