# Chapter 2 — Carolina
## ♩ 110 BPM and below
*🎵 Forró — still playing*

---

The alarm went off at six. I lay there for a moment the way I always do — not asleep, not quite awake, listening to the ocean through the wood slats of my window. After more than a year in this room I still wasn't tired of that sound. Some mornings it was the only reason getting up felt reasonable.

I used the bathroom, splashed water on my face, pulled my hair back. Put on the clothes I'd left on the chair the night before and went downstairs.

The place was mine at this hour. No guests, no sounds except the birds starting up in the trees above and the kitchen coming alive under my hands. Coffee first, always coffee first, then the fruit. Watermelon, papaya, tomatoes cut into chunks. Thick slices of bread for the toast. Butter, jam, condensed milk on the side. By seven the tables were set — crocheted placemats, everything simple and in its place — and I poured myself a cup and sat in one of the low chairs in the garden and had ten minutes of nothing, which was the best ten minutes of the day.

The garden was red earth under the trees, the big ones that held the whole place up, their roots pushing through the ground and their branches holding the thatched roof of the lobby up in the canopy like a nest. A tipi on one side. Steps cut into the base of the largest tree leading up to the rooms above. Flags hanging between the branches, barely moving. In the morning light, before anyone was awake, it was the best place I had ever lived and I knew it.

---

Mati came down at half past seven. He always came down around that time — no alarm I think, just awake when he was awake. He said good morning in castellano and sat at the same table he always sat at, the one facing the garden, and I brought him his coffee without asking.

He ordered two breakfasts. He always ordered two breakfasts. I had stopped asking why.

The English couple came down around nine. The girl first — Amber — then the boy a few minutes behind her, hair still wet, completely unbothered. Ken. He shook my hand when I introduced myself which I liked. Some guests don't bother.

They sat near the garden. I brought them coffee and fruit and eggs. Mati glanced over at them once from his table — brief, the way he glanced at things — and then went back to looking at the garden.

The morning went the way mornings go. By eleven it was done.

---

The French boys didn't make it to breakfast. They'd messaged — arriving today, late flight from Paris. I gave them their key when they arrived mid-afternoon, two twenty-two year olds with big backpacks and the energy of people very happy to have landed somewhere. They dropped everything and went to find the beach.

By evening I was behind the bar cutting limes.

The music was Mati's. He'd asked two nights before if he could have the sound system since the place was empty anyway. He said he couldn't take another night of the same guitar love songs. We got along in castellano and he tipped well so I said yes.

The English couple came in around half past seven. The girl stopped when she came in — the music was too much for her, I could see it before she said anything. She was polite about it. I changed it to forró. She looked relieved. I brought them their drinks and went back to the limes.

Twenty minutes later Mati came down the stairs.

He heard the forró and stopped. Looked at the couple by the fire. Went over to the girl. She said something and he grabbed his chest and fell back in his chair like she'd shot him. The boyfriend said something from the fire. Mati sat down and leaned forward.

I was watching from the bar when he looked over at me.

— Carolina, he said. What music do you like?

I was already moving toward them with the third caipirinha — his, I'd made one without being asked, I'm not sure why — and the question caught me mid-step. The kind of question that needs a simple answer but for some reason my brain and my feet couldn't manage at the same time. I slowed down.

Cumbia, I said. Reggaeton. Forró.

I set his drink down in front of him and straightened up.

He was nodding slowly. The nod of someone who has just had something confirmed.

— Horizontal, he said. All three of them. Different names, different countries, different instruments. Same frequency. Same purpose. You know what that purpose is?

I said I just liked to dance.

---

A few minutes later I saw him take out his phone and open something — not a message, not music, something else. He held it up toward the speaker for a moment. Then he turned it around and showed the table. I couldn't see the number from the bar but I saw Amber lean forward to look at it and the English boy turn in his chair.

Then Mati got up and went to the sound system.

The forró stopped. Something else came on — no voice, no lyrics, just music moving forward with a clean intention, faster than before, steady in a way the forró hadn't been steady. I didn't know what it was. I watched him hold the phone up again and then set it face-up on the table where everyone could see the number on the screen.

One hundred and twenty four.

I kept one eye on the fire all night.

When the French boys wandered in around nine I brought them drinks without being asked and didn't go to bed until it was very late.

Some nights you can just tell.
