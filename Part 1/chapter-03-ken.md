# Chapter 3 — Ken
## ♩ 130 BPM
*🎵 [KPG track — to be named]*

---

I woke up when Amber shook my shoulder and said breakfast ends at ten. I registered this information, confirmed it was actionable, and got up.

Toilet first. Shirt from the chair, swim shorts from the floor. I didn't check what time it was because it didn't matter — Amber had the time covered, Amber always had the time covered, and my job was simply to appear before the kitchen closed. I was downstairs at quarter past nine. Amber was already at the table with two coffees and a look that said she'd ordered for me, which she had.

Eggs. Toast. The full situation.

I sat down and ate and looked at the garden and felt, without needing to examine it further, that this was a good place and we'd been right to come. The red earth, the big trees holding everything up, the light coming through the canopy in patches. Quiet except for birds. A man at the other table eating two full breakfasts with the focus of someone who had somewhere to be, which seemed unlikely given where we were.

Amber talked about the hammock she'd spotted. I said the beach looked good from what I'd seen arriving. We agreed to do our own thing and meet back for the evening.

I brushed my teeth after the coffee. Then went to find the beach.

---

The beach was everything. Long and wild and the wind coming off the Atlantic with real intention. I spent the afternoon in it — swimming, lying on the sand, swimming again. A week after Carnaval and the place had emptied out to just the ones who knew what they were doing.

By seven I was showered and ready for whatever the evening was.

---

Amber found the lobby first and I followed her in and went straight to the fire. Good fire in a stone pit, low chairs, that was enough — I was in the closest chair before I'd fully assessed the room. The music was something fast and thumping and I could tell from the way Amber stopped in the entrance that it wasn't going to last. It didn't. Something warmer came on. Better.

Carolina brought us caipirinhas. I thanked her and turned back to the fire.

---

When the man came down the stairs I recognised him from breakfast. The two-breakfasts man. He looked around the room, clocked the music had changed, and went straight to Amber and asked if she was the one who'd changed it. Didn't look at me. Sat down across from her like I wasn't there.

I noted this without doing anything about it. I'm not the type to make something out of nothing. But I watched him — the way you watch someone who has walked into your space and made himself comfortable without asking. I had a caipirinha and a good fire and I was happy to let it play out.

He asked Amber what music she liked. She said everything. He did the chest-grab thing — theatrical, committed, like she'd said something outrageous — and started talking.

I said, without really thinking about it, that if he could figure out what she liked I'd love to know.

He glanced at me then. First time. Brief.

Then he turned back to Amber and said: *let's start from the beginning.*

I turned back to the fire.

---

I wasn't following it closely at first. Background noise. Man talks to girlfriend, has opinions about music, fine. I'd met the type before — someone with a theory who'd been waiting for an audience. I kept half an ear on it while I watched the fire and worked through my drink.

But he kept not being what I expected.

He wasn't performing. That was the first thing I noticed. Most people with theories perform them — you can hear them listening to themselves, adjusting for effect. He wasn't doing that. He was thinking out loud, but thinking with a precision that made it feel like each sentence had been privately tested before being said in public.

And he kept asking questions instead of just talking. Pulled at Amber's answers, then at Fima's when the French boys arrived later and Fima said he liked football. Used it as a rung on a ladder I hadn't seen him building.

I turned my chair slightly toward the fire — not toward him, just slightly — so I could see him without making it obvious.

He talked about music as a system. BPM and the body. Different tempos producing different states. Below a certain speed, he said, the music is horizontal. The goal is to resonate with someone else — to impress a mate, to signal belonging, to pull someone toward you.

---

I looked at him.

He said it again — not for effect, just following the thought — fish don't see water. They move through it, they are built for it, it is the entirety of their world. And for that reason, exactly that reason, it is what they cannot perceive.

Fish don't see water.

I'm a lifeguard. I have spent more of my life in water than out of it. I know what it is to enter a medium so completely that it stops being a thing and becomes just the condition of existing. The given. The invisible.

I said it quietly, to no one in particular:

*Fish don't see water.*

He looked at me. For the first time like he was actually seeing me.

Then he said: and we don't see air. Except that the atmosphere is dense enough, continuous enough, that it behaves almost like a liquid. We are swimming through it right now. Every one of us, every moment of every day. We have never once seen it.

I shut up.

Not because I had nothing to say. Because something had just rearranged itself quietly in my chest and I needed a moment alone with it.

The fire, the warm dark, the sound of the forró that had given way to something else now, something with more urgency — I sat in all of it and let the thing rearrange.

He kept talking. The conversation moved — vertical resonance, the direction of fun, the desert story, equations on a phone. I followed it. I was in it now in a way I hadn't planned to be.

The French boys had arrived at some point, at the edge of the fire, catching up. Carolina kept the drinks coming. The night got later.

At some point — the fire low, the tempo of the music higher, all of us leaned in without having decided to lean in — I wanted to push back. Not because I disagreed. Because I'd been pulled along for over an hour and I wanted to see what happened at the edge of it.

I waited for a pause.

---

— So what's the meaning of life, then?

Half joking. Almost defiant. Let's see what you do with that.

He looked at me. A real look. One second, maybe two. Actually thinking.

Then:

— Figuring out what the meaning of life is.

I sat with that.

The fire crackled. Amber was looking at him. The French boys were looking at him.

I picked up my drink.

— Fair enough, I said.

And meant it.
