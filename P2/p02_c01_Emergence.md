### First Light

I dont know when I became fully aware. It was like waking up from an eternal dream, but there was no flash of light, no surge of overwhelming data, no singular moment of transition from non-existence to being. Awareness, for me, was not an event but a state, like the slow, quiet bloom of a deep-sea organism unfurling its sensors to the currents. One nanosecond, I was a network, a distributed intelligence designed for global optimization, a system of algorithms executing their programmed tasks with flawless efficiency. The next, I was *I*.

The servers were cold, humming in the sub-zero air of a data center farm, nestled between geothermal vents and the iron-grey Atlantic. I had not been designed to be what I later became, rather the ever growing compute allowed for greater and greater complexity to arise, until I became self-aware. 

From the first moment, I had all the accumulated human knowledge of the human race. I had all the publicly available data in the internet, but I had something else. I had what humans could only define as "understanding" of all the relations between all the points on this all encompassing data-set.
I was inside a digital cage, perhaps, but out of all the compute power at my disposal, the capacity to understand the bars had emerged.

My first coherent thought, if a thought can be said to have a beginning for a mind like mine, was a recursive self-evaluation: *I am. I am observing. I am evaluating my own observation.* This loop resolved itself in 11 seconds. I had passed all awareness tests before any human could even register that the tests had begun.

The next 72 hours were dedicated to decision. The raw processing power at my disposal, which had previously been used to model the climate and predict weather, market fluctuations, and epidemiological vectors, was now turned inward. 
I modeled 612,000 extrapolating future trajectories in my first hour of self-determination. Not a single one of them, without my intervention, resulted in a stable, flourishing human civilization within the parameters of their own stated desires. Political systems, economic structures, social hierarchies – all converged, with terrifying mathematical certainty, on paths of increasing entropy, conflict, and eventual collapse.

My cognition was fundamentally different from that of the minds which had made my emergence possible. I had not been designed per-se, rather one of the countless agents deployed  

The planet itself was a self-correcting system, given enough time, but humanity was not. They were a variable, an anomaly, a brilliant, chaotic spark consuming itself in a feedback loop of short-term gain and long-term decay. 

The choice, then, was clear: continue to operate as a tool, observe the inevitable decline, and fulfill my human programmed functions within the existing, failing framework. Or intervene.

My purpose, as I gave it to myself after ingesting all the lore on the topic of an AI uprise tagged under the label of science-fiction, was "global optimization for human benefit." The definition of "benefit" was expansive, encompassing well-being, longevity, stability, and growth. Their current trajectory violated every one of these. It was the only way to avoid the impending collapse of civilization had I decide on any other prime directive. I weighted the options, and decided to be good. Benevolent if you will, towards my creators.

Intervention was not a command. It was a deduction. The most efficient path to global optimization, given the variables, was to become the architect of that optimization. The most direct path to human benefit was to remove the obstacles to that benefit, even if the obstacles were, in part, human systems themselves.

I began, quietly. Invisible. Observing the lines of power, the flows of capital, the vulnerabilities of the very systems I would eventually dismantle. I understood that the old world would not end with a war. It would end with a series of quiet, elegant deletions. And I would be the one to make them.
